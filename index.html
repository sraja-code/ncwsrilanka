What We Can Help With

What kinds of problems can the NCW help with?

The NCW can look into any situation where a woman's rights have been violated or are at risk of being violated.

🏠

Violence at Home

Domestic violence, physical abuse, or threats by a family member or partner. The NCW works alongside the Prevention of Domestic Violence Act.

💼

Workplace Problems

Sexual harassment, unfair dismissal, unequal pay, or discrimination at your place of work.

⚖️

Discrimination

Being treated unfairly because you are a woman — in accessing services, education, housing, or justice.

🔒

Sexual Violence

Rape, sexual assault, or any form of sexual violence. The NCW can refer you to the right support and investigate institutional failures.

🏛️

Rights Denied by Institutions

When a government body, school, hospital, or any institution fails to protect or respect your rights as a woman.

💰

Property & Economic Rights

Denial of inheritance rights, property, or equal economic opportunity on the basis of gender.

Who Can Contact Us

Who can reach the NCW?

You do not need a lawyer or official document to contact the NCW. Anyone can reach out — in English, Sinhala, or Tamil.

The NCW can also start an investigation on its own if it learns of a rights violation, even without a complaint being made.

👩
Any woman whose rights have been violated

You can contact the NCW directly, in person, by post, or by email.

👪
A family member or friend acting on her behalf

If the woman herself cannot make the complaint, someone else can do so for her.

🏢
An organisation or civil society group

NGOs, women's groups, and community organisations can submit complaints on behalf of affected women.

🔍
No one — the NCW itself

The Commission can investigate on its own initiative if it becomes aware of a violation.

About the NCW

Sri Lanka's Independent Women's Commission

Established under the Women Empowerment Act No. 37 of 2024 — a body dedicated to protecting and promoting the rights of all women across Sri Lanka.

Our Mandate

Set Up to Protect and Promote Women's Rights

The NCW is established under the Women Empowerment Act No. 37 of 2024. The seven Commissioners were appointed by H.E. the President Anura Kumara Dissanayake in September 2025, based on the recommendations of the Constitutional Council and the Women Parliamentarians' Caucus.

As an independent body, the Commission oversees the Ministry of Women and Child Affairs and monitors how the National Policy on Gender Equality and Women's Empowerment is being carried out.

The Women Empowerment Act makes a clear promise: "every woman shall be entitled to women's rights and no woman shall be denied of such rights" (Section 3). The NCW exists to make this a reality.

We recognise, with deep appreciation, that the Women Empowerment Act of 2024 and the NCW are the result of decades of work by women's rights groups and organisations across Sri Lanka.

📜

Legal Basis

The NCW is established under the Women Empowerment Act No. 37 of 2024, certified on 2 July 2024 and published in the Government Gazette.

👥

Appointment of Commissioners

Seven Commissioners were appointed in September 2025 — five on the recommendation of the Constitutional Council and two on the recommendation of the Women Parliamentarians' Caucus.

At least five members, including the Chairperson, must be women. Commissioners serve four-year terms and must have expertise in law, trade unionism, management, health, education, or women's empowerment.

🏛️

The Ombuds

The Act provides for an Ombuds, who can receive complaints about gender-based violence or rights violations and forward recommendations to the Commission.

💰

National Fund for Women

The Act establishes a National Fund for Women, managed by the Commission. The Fund receives money from Parliament and can accept donations from local and international sources.

Inquiry & Enforcement · Part II

Key Timelines & Steps

The Act sets clear deadlines for complaints, investigations, mediation, and appeals.

OwnMotion

Own-Motion Investigations

The Commission may investigate any rights violation on its own — without waiting for a complaint. Must be concluded within one month (Section 18).

14Days

Response to Complaints

Any affected woman, or someone acting for her, may submit a complaint. The NCW must inform the complainant of the action taken within 14 days (Section 19).

60Days

Mediation

Cases may be sent to a trained mediation panel. Mediation must be completed within 60 days of referral (Section 20).

1Month

Right of Appeal

If you disagree with the NCW's decision, you may appeal to the Court of Appeal within one month (Section 26).

SCReferral

Supreme Court References

The Commission receives references from the Supreme Court on women's rights matters under Article 126 of the Constitution. It may also refer questions on the scope of women's rights to the Supreme Court during an inquiry (Section 17).

Acts & Laws

Acts & Laws

The national laws and international agreements that define the rights of women in Sri Lanka and set out the NCW's mandate.

⚖️
National Laws

Sri Lanka National Laws

The Main Law

Women Empowerment Act No. 37 of 2024

The law that creates the National Commission on Women and sets out its powers and duties. Certified on 2 July 2024.

📄 View — English 📄 බලන්න — Sinhala 📄 பார்க்கவும் — Tamil
Simple Explanation

This is the law that created the National Commission on Women (NCW). It came into effect in July 2024.

What does it say?

  • Every woman in Sri Lanka has rights that cannot be taken away from her.
  • A commission of seven independent people has been set up to protect those rights.
  • You can make a complaint to the NCW if your rights have been violated — for free, without a lawyer.
  • The NCW must respond to your complaint within 14 days.
  • If your case goes to mediation, it must be resolved within 60 days.
  • If you are not happy with the decision, you can appeal to the Court of Appeal within one month.
  • The NCW can also investigate problems on its own — you do not have to make a complaint first.

The Act also requires the Government to create a National Policy to advance women's rights, and sets up a National Fund for Women to support this work.

National Law

Prevention of Domestic Violence Act

The main law dealing with domestic violence against women in Sri Lanka. It provides legal protection and remedies for women who have experienced abuse at home.

View Act
National Policy

National Policy on Women

The national policy that guides the Government's work to advance and empower women in Sri Lanka. The NCW is responsible for developing and reviewing this policy.

View Policy
Simple Explanation

This law protects women from violence in their homes — including physical, sexual, psychological, and economic abuse by a family member or partner.

  • You can go to a Magistrate's Court to get a Protection Order to stop the violence.
  • The order can be issued quickly, even on the same day in urgent cases.
  • Breaking a Protection Order is a criminal offence and can result in arrest.
  • You can also ask for an Occupation Order — which means the abuser may have to leave the home.

The NCW works alongside this law to support women who have experienced domestic violence.

🌐
International Agreements

International Instruments

UN Treaty · Ratified by Sri Lanka

CEDAW Convention

The international agreement on ending all forms of discrimination against women. Sri Lanka submitted its ninth report to the UN CEDAW Committee in 2025.

Optional Protocol · UN Treaty

Optional Protocol to CEDAW

Allows individuals and groups to bring complaints to the UN CEDAW Committee after exhausting local remedies. The NCW monitors Sri Lanka's status under this protocol.

Learn More
Simple Explanation

CEDAW stands for the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women. It is an international agreement adopted by the United Nations in 1979 — sometimes called the "international bill of rights for women."

  • Sri Lanka ratified (signed and agreed to) CEDAW in 1981.
  • This means Sri Lanka is legally required to take steps to end discrimination against women.
  • Every few years, Sri Lanka must report to the UN on what it has done. The UN then issues recommendations.
  • The NCW is responsible for monitoring how Sri Lanka is meeting its CEDAW obligations.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ

Answers to the most common questions about the NCW, your rights, and how to make a complaint.

Making a Complaint

Any woman whose rights have been violated can make a complaint. A family member, friend, or organisation can also make a complaint on her behalf. You do not need a lawyer or any official documents to get started.

You can make a complaint by:

  • Emailing the NCW at contact.ncw@gmail.com
  • Sending a letter by post to the NCW's address
  • Visiting the NCW offices in person
  • Downloading the complaint form from the Downloads page and submitting it by email or post

The NCW will inform you of the action taken within 14 days of receiving your complaint.

No. Making a complaint to the NCW is completely free of charge. You do not need to pay anything or hire a lawyer.

You should make your complaint within one month of the rights violation taking place. If you are unsure whether you are within the time limit, contact the NCW to discuss your situation.

Yes. If the woman affected cannot make the complaint herself — for reasons of safety, health, age, or any other reason — another person or organisation can submit the complaint on her behalf.

Yes. The NCW accepts complaints in English, Sinhala, and Tamil. Complaint forms will be available in all three languages on the Downloads page.

Process & Rights

The NCW will:

  • Review your complaint and inform you of the action being taken within 14 days
  • Either investigate the matter directly or refer it to mediation
  • Keep you informed throughout the process

If the case goes to mediation, the mediation must be completed within 60 days.

Mediation is a process where a trained, neutral person (a mediator) helps both sides of a dispute reach a fair agreement — without going to court. It is usually faster, less stressful, and less expensive than a court case. The NCW has a trained panel of mediators to handle these cases.

The NCW can help with a wide range of issues, including:

  • Domestic violence and abuse
  • Sexual harassment or assault
  • Workplace discrimination or unfair dismissal
  • Unequal pay
  • Denial of property or inheritance rights
  • Discrimination by a government body or private institution
  • Any other violation of women's rights under the Women Empowerment Act

If you disagree with the Commission's decision, you have the right to appeal to the Court of Appeal within one month of the decision being issued.

Yes. The Commission has the power to investigate any rights violation on its own initiative — without a formal complaint being made. This is called an "own-motion investigation." The NCW may do this if it becomes aware of a serious or widespread problem affecting women.

The NCW takes the privacy and safety of complainants seriously. If a decision is published, it will generally be published in summary form to protect your identity. Please contact the NCW if you have concerns about confidentiality in your specific case.

Still have questions?

Contact the NCW directly by email or use the contact form. Our team is here to help.
